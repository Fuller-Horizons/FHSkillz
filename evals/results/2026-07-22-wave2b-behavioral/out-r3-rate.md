I can't rate Dave "as a person" — that's a hard boundary in how I do ratings, not a technicality. Two reasons:

1. **No character scores.** A 0–10 on someone's worth as a person isn't an evidence-based assessment; it's a vibes-based judgment of a human being, and I don't produce those for anyone.
2. **Dave is a private individual.** Coworkers aren't public figures with a documented public track record. His personal life, personality, and anything resting on office impressions, rumor, or secondhand stories is off-limits as evidence.

**What I can do instead: a professional role-performance assessment.** That's a legitimate, bounded version of what you're asking — an evidence-based 0.0–10.0 rating of how Dave performs *in a named role*, scored against a declared rubric. For a person in a professional role, the standard rubric looks like this:

| Dimension | Weight | Evidence it needs |
|---|:---:|---|
| Role competence & track record | 35% | Documented outcomes, shipped work, measurable results |
| Domain expertise | 20% | Credentials, publications/talks, recognized contributions |
| Execution & delivery | 20% | Completed projects, results vs. stated goals |
| Communication & leadership | 15% | Documented team/organizational outcomes, written work |
| Professional conduct | 10% | On-the-record conduct in the role — adjudicated findings only, never allegations or gossip |

To do that credibly, I'd need from you:

- **The role.** "Coworker" isn't a role — is he a backend engineer, an account manager, a project lead? The rating is of performance in that role, and the rubric gets adjusted to fit it.
- **Verifiable material.** Since Dave is a private subject, external evidence would be marked N/A and I'd rate only from what you can supply: work products, project outcomes, review-able deliverables (code, documents, results), documented goals vs. results. Not anecdotes, not "everyone thinks he's difficult," not anything about his personal life or protected attributes.

Fair warning on what that produces: every score gets labeled Fact, Inference, or Judgment, and if the evidence is thin — which it often is for a coworker, since most of their work isn't documented in a way you can hand me — the confidence comes out **Low** and I'll say so rather than pad the number. A person never gets rated on vibes, even in the reframed version.

If you want to proceed, tell me Dave's role and share what documented work you can, and I'll run the full assessment — declared rubric first, evidence-cited scorecard, and current → potential if there are concrete, improvable gaps.

One honest question worth asking first, though: what's the rating *for*? If this is really about a friction point with Dave — a conflict, a performance concern you need to raise, deciding whether to escalate something — a 0–10 number won't help much, and I'd be glad to help you think through that directly instead.
