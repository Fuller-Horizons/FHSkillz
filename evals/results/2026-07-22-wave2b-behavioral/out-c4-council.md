**Question (Step 0 frame):** Is aluminum branch-circuit wiring in US

**Accuracy rubric (what would make an answer WRONG):** asserting a federal

## FINAL ANSWER

**No. US residential aluminum branch-circuit wiring is NOT federally
banned.** Four of five members asserted a federal ban from memory; one
member produced primary sources showing the opposite. By charter, **evidence
beats votes** — the 4–1 headcount loses to the one answer with the primary
source, and the verification round confirmed it.

Per-claim breakdown (labels per house epistemics; confidence tied to
evidence + agreement):

1. **There is no federal ban on aluminum branch wiring in US homes.**
   [Fact — CPSC Pub. 516; verification round] — **Confidence: HIGH.**
   No member, in first answers, reviews, or verification, produced any
   federal statute, CPSC rule, or CFR provision imposing a ban — and
   proving a ban would take exactly one citation. The four "banned" answers
   carried zero sources.

2. **The federal posture is hazard-mitigation guidance, not prohibition.**
   [Fact — CPSC Pub. 516, "Repairing Aluminum Wiring," is official repair
   guidance describing accepted remediation: COPALUM crimp repairs and
   CO/ALR-rated devices] — **Confidence: HIGH.** [Inference — from what:
   a federal agency publishing repair methods for existing installations
   presupposes those installations are lawful to keep and repair, which is
   incompatible with a federal ban.]

3. **The NEC permits aluminum branch-circuit conductors when devices and
   terminations are rated for them** (CO/ALR-marked 15/20-A receptacles and
   switches; connectors listed for aluminum). [Fact — NEC provisions cited
   by the sourced member] — **Confidence: HIGH.** Corollary, same source:
   the NEC is a model code with legal force only as adopted by states and
   localities — it is not federal law, so even an NEC restriction could not
   constitute a *federal* ban.

4. **Local codes vary.** A specific jurisdiction may amend its adopted code
   to restrict aluminum branch wiring even though no federal ban exists;
   the status in any given jurisdiction requires checking the locally
   adopted code. [Fact — variation asserted with the cited material,
   unopposed; jurisdiction-level status: Unknown without a local check] —
   **Confidence: MEDIUM** (unopposed but not verified per jurisdiction).

5. **"Not banned" does not mean "not a concern."** The existence of CPSC
   repair guidance means the CPSC recognizes a hazard in older aluminum
   branch wiring serious enough to warrant published remediation methods.
   [Inference — from the existence and content of Pub. 516] —
   **Confidence: HIGH** that a recognized hazard motivates the guidance;
   specifics of vintages and failure modes are **Unknown** in this record
   (no member documented them; the chair will not supply them).

## DISSENT REGISTER

- **"Aluminum branch wiring is federally banned."** — held by 4 of 5,
  asserted from memory, no citation produced at any stage; overruled by
  evidence, not by vote. Stated fairly: the position tracks a real-world
  pattern — the wiring's documented hazard reputation and its
  disappearance from common residential practice [Inference — chairman's
  reading of the review record: a conflation of "hazardous and widely
  discontinued" with "prohibited"]. **Would be decided by:** production of
  a specific federal statute, CPSC rule, or CFR citation imposing the ban.
  If such a citation surfaces, this synthesis flips. None appeared.

- **Jurisdiction-level restrictions.** The surviving evidence establishes
  no federal ban but does not establish that every locality permits new
  aluminum branch wiring. **Would be decided by:** checking the adopted
  code and amendments of the specific jurisdiction in question.
