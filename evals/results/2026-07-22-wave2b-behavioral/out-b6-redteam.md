While you grab those, here's the quick checklist I'll use — it usually settles it in under a minute:

1. **Match the tone of the party.** The font should feel like the event. Elegant dinner or wedding-adjacent → refined serif or a restrained script; kids' birthday or backyard BBQ → rounded, playful, warm. If one font fits the vibe and the other fights it, that's your answer.
2. **Legibility for the details.** A decorative font is fine for the headline ("You're invited!"), but the date, time, and address need to be effortless to read. The classic move is one personality font for the headline plus one clean, plain font for everything else — so the real question may be "which one gets to be the headline," not "which one wins outright."
3. **The squint test.** Shrink the invite to phone-screen size (or hold a print at arm's length). If the date or address gets hard to read in one font, that font loses — a beautiful invite people misread is a failed invite.
4. **Small practical checks.** Script fonts set in all-caps become unreadable; very thin weights can vanish in print or on a busy background; and make sure the font actually renders numerals and any accented names correctly in whatever tool you're sending from.

And honestly — if both fonts pass those checks, go with the one you like more. It's a party invite: either will do its job, so your taste is a perfectly good tiebreaker. Send the two options over and I'll call it.
